Storm Shear, Updraft & Mesocyclone Calculator

About This Calculator

This tool estimates three key storm parameters:

  • Updraft Speed (w): How fast the storm air is rising (in m/s). We calculate this using CAPE (Convective Available Potential Energy), which measures the energy available for storm development.
  • Effective Advective Wind (Ueff): The average wind speed that pushes the storm sideways—estimated from the observed tilt of the storm.
  • Bulk Wind Shear (S): How quickly the wind speed increases with height. We assume a default storm “height” (vertical separation) of 6000 m, meaning the wind increases from near zero at the surface to about twice the effective wind at the top.

In simpler terms, if you know the energy available in the atmosphere (CAPE) and notice a storm leaning at a certain angle, this tool estimates both how hard the storm is being lifted and pushed sideways, as well as how quickly that wind changes with height.

Mesocyclones and Storm Types

A mesocyclone is a rotating updraft commonly found within supercell thunderstorms. They’re a key feature that meteorologists look at when forecasting severe weather.

Supercells are organized thunderstorms known for their persistent, rotating updrafts. They tend to produce large hail, damaging winds, and sometimes tornadoes.

In contrast, popcorn storms (or pulse storms) are scattered, short-lived cells that pop up in clusters. These storms generally have weak, non-rotating updrafts and are less likely to produce severe weather.

How to Field Test These Parameters

When you’re out in the field with a supercell, you can gather some of these measurements using:

  • CAPE: Estimated from weather balloon soundings or local observations. Higher CAPE means more energy for a strong updraft.
  • Storm Tilt Angle: Visually or via radar—if the storm appears noticeably slanted, you can measure that angle or estimate it.
  • Wind Shear: Often measured between low and mid/high altitudes using mobile mesonet data or remote sensing. In this calculator, we assume a default 6000 m separation for simplicity.

By combining these observations, you can classify the storm. A supercell with significant tilt and high CAPE is more likely to develop a mesocyclone than scattered, short-lived “popcorn” storms.
